# Press Release Writing Tips: How to Get Journalists to Actually Read Your Story

**Author:** Christopher Lembke, Founder of UpsurgeIQ  
**Published:** December 21, 2025  
**Reading Time:** 7 minutes  
**Category:** Public Relations, Content Writing, Media Relations

---

Every day, journalists receive hundreds of press releases competing for their attention. Most get deleted within seconds, never making it past the subject line. The difference between press releases that generate coverage and those that disappear into digital oblivion often comes down to fundamental writing principles that many businesses overlook. Crafting an effective press release requires more than following a template—it demands understanding what journalists need, respecting their time constraints, and delivering information in a format that makes their job easier rather than harder.

## Start with a Compelling Headline

Your headline serves as the gatekeeper determining whether anyone reads further. Journalists scanning their inbox make split-second decisions based on subject lines and headlines, filtering out anything that doesn't immediately signal relevance and news value. A weak headline dooms even the most newsworthy announcement to obscurity, while a strong one can elevate a modest story into something worth covering.

Effective headlines communicate the core news clearly and concisely without resorting to hype or clickbait tactics that undermine credibility. They answer the fundamental question: "What happened that matters?" Rather than vague statements like "Company Announces Exciting New Development," specific headlines like "Manufacturing Firm Cuts Production Costs 40% with Renewable Energy Transition" immediately convey concrete news that journalists can evaluate.

Numbers, data points, and specific outcomes strengthen headlines by providing tangible evidence of significance. Journalists gravitate toward quantifiable claims they can verify and reference in their coverage. Headlines featuring percentages, dollar amounts, timeframes, or scale indicators ("First," "Largest," "Only") signal substantive news rather than promotional fluff.

Active voice and strong verbs create urgency and clarity. Passive constructions like "New Product Has Been Launched" feel bureaucratic and lifeless compared to "Tech Startup Launches AI Tool That Reduces Customer Service Costs by Half." The latter immediately communicates who did what and why it matters, giving journalists everything they need to assess news value at a glance.

## Lead with the Most Important Information

The inverted pyramid structure—placing the most newsworthy information first and supporting details later—remains the gold standard for press release writing. This approach serves both journalists working under tight deadlines and readers who may only skim the opening paragraphs. Your first paragraph must answer the essential questions: Who, What, When, Where, Why, and How.

Many press releases bury their lead beneath company background, industry context, or promotional language. This forces journalists to hunt for the actual news, wasting time they don't have. The opening sentence should deliver your core message with clarity and impact. If a journalist reads nothing beyond your first paragraph, they should still understand your announcement and its significance.

The second and third paragraphs expand on your lead, providing additional context, supporting data, and relevant details that flesh out the story. This is where you explain the broader implications, connect your announcement to industry trends, or provide background that helps journalists understand why this matters now. Each paragraph should be able to stand alone, allowing editors to cut from the bottom without losing essential information—a common practice when space is limited.

Avoid the temptation to front-load your press release with company history or background information. While this context may feel important internally, it's rarely newsworthy and pushes your actual announcement further down the page. Save company background for a boilerplate section at the end, where journalists can reference it if needed without it interfering with your news.

## Use Clear, Jargon-Free Language

Industry jargon, technical terminology, and corporate speak create barriers between your message and your audience. Journalists writing for general audiences need to understand your announcement well enough to explain it to readers who lack specialized knowledge. Press releases laden with acronyms, buzzwords, and insider terminology force journalists to do extra research or simply move on to clearer stories.

This doesn't mean dumbing down complex topics—it means explaining them accessibly. Define technical terms when first introduced, use analogies that connect unfamiliar concepts to common experiences, and prioritize clarity over sounding sophisticated. A press release about "leveraging synergistic solutions to optimize stakeholder value propositions" communicates nothing, while "helping businesses reduce costs while improving customer satisfaction" conveys a clear value proposition anyone can understand.

Specific examples and concrete details make abstract concepts tangible. Rather than claiming your product "enhances operational efficiency," explain that it "reduces invoice processing time from three days to three hours." Journalists can visualize and communicate specific outcomes far more effectively than vague benefits. These details also provide the concrete material that makes stories compelling and credible.

Short sentences and paragraphs improve readability and comprehension. Dense blocks of text discourage reading, while concise, well-structured prose respects journalists' time and cognitive load. Aim for sentences averaging 15-20 words and paragraphs no longer than 3-4 sentences. This creates visual breathing room and makes your press release easier to scan and digest quickly.

## Include Meaningful Quotes

Quotes humanize press releases and provide journalists with ready-made material they can incorporate directly into their coverage. However, most press release quotes fail to serve this purpose, instead offering generic praise or restating information already covered in the body text. Effective quotes provide perspective, insight, or emotion that factual reporting alone cannot convey.

The best quotes reveal why your announcement matters from a human perspective. Rather than having your CEO say "We're excited to announce this new product," have them explain the problem it solves: "For years, small businesses told us they couldn't afford enterprise-level security. We built this specifically to give them protection that was previously accessible only to Fortune 500 companies." This quote provides context, demonstrates customer understanding, and explains the announcement's significance.

Quotes should sound like actual human speech, not corporate messaging. Read them aloud—if they sound stilted or unnatural, rewrite them. Journalists can immediately recognize manufactured quotes and may skip them entirely or request fresh ones, creating extra work for everyone. Authentic-sounding quotes are more likely to appear in coverage unchanged.

Strategic quote attribution enhances credibility and provides multiple perspectives. Include quotes from your executive leadership to establish authority and vision, from customers to demonstrate real-world impact, and from industry experts or partners to provide third-party validation. This variety gives journalists options for different story angles and adds depth to your narrative.

## Provide Supporting Evidence and Context

Journalists need verifiable facts to support their coverage. Unsupported claims, no matter how compelling, create liability and credibility risks that responsible reporters avoid. Your press release should include concrete evidence—data, research findings, customer testimonials, or expert endorsements—that substantiates your key messages and gives journalists confidence in your story.

Original research or proprietary data significantly strengthens press releases by providing exclusive information journalists cannot find elsewhere. If you've conducted customer surveys, analyzed industry trends, or compiled performance metrics, include the most compelling findings. These statistics often become the hook that makes your story newsworthy even if your core announcement is relatively modest.

Third-party validation from credible sources adds weight to your claims. Industry analyst commentary, customer success metrics, partnership announcements with recognized brands, or awards from respected organizations all provide external verification that your announcement merits attention. These elements transform a self-promotional announcement into a story with independent corroboration.

Contextualizing your announcement within broader industry trends or societal shifts elevates it from company news to a data point in a larger narrative. Explain how your announcement reflects changing customer needs, responds to regulatory developments, or addresses emerging challenges in your sector. This framing helps journalists understand why their audience should care and provides the broader context that makes stories more compelling.

## Format for Easy Scanning and Use

Visual presentation affects how journalists engage with your press release. Dense, poorly formatted text discourages reading, while clean, well-organized layouts facilitate quick comprehension and information extraction. Professional formatting signals that you respect journalists' time and understand media conventions.

Use descriptive subheadings to break up text and guide readers through your content. These signposts allow journalists to quickly locate specific information without reading every word. Subheadings like "Key Features," "Availability and Pricing," or "Industry Impact" help readers navigate to the sections most relevant to their needs.

Bullet points work well for listing features, specifications, or key facts that don't require narrative explanation. However, use them judiciously—entire press releases formatted as bullet points feel lazy and force journalists to construct the narrative themselves. Reserve bullets for genuinely list-appropriate content while maintaining paragraph structure for your main story.

Include all essential contact information, multimedia assets, and supplementary resources at the end of your release. Journalists should be able to quickly find spokesperson contact details, high-resolution images, video links, or additional background documents without searching. Make these resources easily accessible through direct links rather than requiring email requests that create friction.

## Timing and Distribution Strategy

Even perfectly written press releases fail if distributed at the wrong time or through inappropriate channels. Strategic timing considers news cycles, industry events, and audience availability to maximize your announcement's visibility and relevance. Understanding when journalists are most receptive and least overwhelmed improves your chances of breaking through the noise.

Avoid major holidays, weekends, and late Friday afternoons when newsrooms operate with skeleton staffs and audiences are less engaged. Tuesday through Thursday mornings typically offer the best windows for distribution, giving journalists time to develop stories for current or next-day publication. Monitor your industry's news cycle to avoid releasing during major events that will overshadow your announcement.

Embargo strategies can build anticipation and give journalists time to develop more thoughtful coverage, but they require careful execution. Only embargo genuinely significant news that warrants advance notice, clearly communicate embargo terms, and honor them strictly. Breaking your own embargo destroys credibility and ensures journalists ignore future requests.

Targeted distribution to relevant journalists and publications generates better results than mass distribution to generic media lists. Research which reporters cover your industry, understand their beats and recent coverage, and personalize your pitch to explain why your story fits their focus. This approach requires more effort but dramatically improves response rates and coverage quality.
